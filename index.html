Institutionalising Research Software Recognition

Transferable Policy Design from Monash Business School

Dianne Cook, Rob Hyndman, Tom Honeyman

Department of Econometrics and Business Statistics

2026-03-06

Structural Context

In computationally intensive disciplines, software frequently constitutes the operational form of research methods.

Despite this:

  • Academic promotion systems remain publication-centric
  • Software contributions are often evaluated informally or not at all

This case examines a faculty-level intervention designed to align incentive structures with contemporary research practice.

The Recognition Gap

Despite clear research value:

  • Software rarely counted formally in promotion
  • Often assessed informally or case-by-case
  • No consistent peer-review framework equivalent to journals

Position articulated within Monash Business School:

  • Software should be treated as a research output
  • It should count alongside traditional outputs in promotion cases

The Monash Intervention

Scope:

Implemented within Monash Business School (faculty-level change)

Process:

  • Sustained advocacy over several years
  • Change in faculty leadership created opportunity
  • A concise, one-page policy drafted
  • Reviewed and approved through Faculty Research Committee

Key point:

Change did not require university-wide reform. It began at the faculty level.

Policy Design and Evaluation Mechanism (1/2)

Core principle:

Research software is recognised as a research output for promotion and integrated into the existing points-based evaluation framework.

Structural design:

  • Software assessed separately from papers
  • Allocated 0–3 points, aligned with journal scoring bands
  • Evaluated through a formal peer-review process embedded in faculty HR governance

Governance and review process:

  • Committee convened by the Deputy Dean (Research)
  • Rotating discipline experts (internal and external)
  • At least one anonymous external review per submission
  • Two external reviews required where no prior peer review exists
  • Conflict-of-interest rules applied
  • Brief written feedback provided

Policy Design and Evaluation Mechanism (2/2)

Submission requirements and quality standards:

Applicants must provide:

  • Version-controlled source code (e.g. GitHub)
  • Evidence of documentation and reproducibility
  • Clear description of individual contribution
  • Evidence of user impact or uptake
  • Evidence of new features, improvements, or sustained maintenance (for ongoing projects)

Assessment standards parallel journal expectations:

  • Method documentation at publication level
  • Preference for prototype tools or research software infrastructure over analysis code
  • Versioning and citable releases
  • Mechanism for bug reporting and contributions
  • Clear maintainership and governance model

This model embeds software recognition within formal quality control and HR mechanisms, rather than treating it as symbolic inclusion.

Structural Shift in Evaluation

Significance:

The shift is not symbolic recognition, but movement from discretionary consideration to institutionalised category with procedural legitimacy.

System-level effect:

  • Policies of this kind shape the long-term composition of a faculty or school.
  • By formally recognising software, the institution signals what kinds of scholarly labour are valued.
  • Over time, this influences hiring, retention, and promotion trajectories.
  • The policy may attract and retain academics whose profiles combine methodological innovation, engineering practice, and community stewardship.
  • Incentive reform therefore has selection effects: surfacing software as legitimate scholarship contributes to shaping the type of academic workforce an institution cultivates.
  • Faculty-wide structural recognition rather than ad hoc approval
  • Moves from individual advocacy to institutional policy
  • Aligns incentives with actual research practice in computational fields

Potential benefits:

  • Incentivises high-quality research software
  • Supports stable career progression
  • Signals cultural shift in evaluation norms
  • May attract researchers with strong software credentials

Potential Open Questions

1. Heterogeneity of Software Contributions

  • Some submissions represent short, sharp methodological innovations that shift practice quickly within a field.
  • Others reflect sustained governance and long-term maintenance of large, widely used software frameworks.
  • Time horizon
  • Visibility of impact
  • Type of labour (innovation vs stewardship)
  • Community dependence

2. Commensurability Challenge

A core policy question:

  • How do you make equivalent — within a single promotion points system — very different types of software effort?
  • Is a breakthrough prototype equivalent to a decade of responsible maintenance of critical infrastructure?
  • Should impact be measured through novelty, scale of adoption, longevity, or ecosystem stability?

3. Incentive Effects

  • Does the framework unintentionally favour visible innovation over invisible maintenance?
  • How do we ensure governance and stewardship work is not undervalued relative to new feature development?

Positioning Within OECD Policy Dimensions

This case study sits primarily within the Human Resources and Motivation dimension of this policy workshop’s framework.

Human Resources and Motivation

  • Aligns academic promotion incentives with software production
  • Integrates evaluation into existing HR promotion mechanisms
  • Uses peer review to support transparency, fairness, and staff motivation

Standards, Assessment, Certification, and Alignment

  • Establishes a formal faculty-level governance and review structure
  • Applies quality standards comparable to journal evaluation
  • Requires documentation, version control, and evidence of impact

This is not primarily an infrastructure intervention; it is an incentive-structure intervention.

From the Monash case:

  1. Faculty-level change is feasible.
  2. Policy can be concise and targeted.
  3. Peer review is essential for legitimacy.
  4. Alignment with existing evaluation systems reduces friction.
  5. Cultural change often precedes formal policy change.

Key question for institutions and policymakers

What behaviours do current promotion systems incentivise — and are those behaviours aligned with the realities of computational research?
